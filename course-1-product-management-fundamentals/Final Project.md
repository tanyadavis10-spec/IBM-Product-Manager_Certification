## Scenario Summary

StreamLean Gaming is a Silicon Valley edtech startup led by CEO Claire Henderson. The company built its reputation developing educational math games for children but hit a growth plateau as competitors introduced new ideas. Claire decided to formalize product development by building a dedicated product management team — bringing in Product Manager Emily Dawson, Project Manager Michael Chen, and Marketing Manager Sarah Rodriguez — to develop a new generation of math-based learning activities that would differentiate StreamLean in the market.

**The Team:**
- **Emily Dawson (Product Manager)** — educational game designer, owned the product vision
- **Michael Chen (Project Manager)** — led implementation, managed timelines and budget
- **Sarah Rodriguez (Marketing Manager)** — led market research and go-to-market strategy
- **Mark Robinson (VP of Innovation)** — assembled the team and defined the mission

---

## What Went Well — 7 Observations

| # | Observation |
|---|---|
| 1 | Assembled a product management team with the right skills and experience for the specific product domain |
| 2 | Conducted thorough market research — visiting schools, running focus groups, and entering math competitions to understand what made games successful |
| 3 | Michael created a detailed project plan that kept the team coordinated and on track throughout development |
| 4 | Strong cross-functional collaboration across product, engineering, design, QA, and marketing |
| 5 | Emily created a clear product vision — an interactive adventure world where math problems unlock hidden treasures — that kept the team aligned on the goal |
| 6 | Sarah targeted the right audience — educators and parents — through educational conferences and influencer outreach |
| 7 | The team maintained a consistent focus on delivering customer value by grounding every decision in educational standards and learner engagement |

---

## What Could Have Gone Better — 5 Observations

| # | Observation |
|---|---|
| 1 | The scope of the product was not clearly defined upfront — competitive features like class vs. class competition were added during design without a formal scoping process |
| 2 | Customer feedback was gathered primarily through research before development but lacked a structured ongoing loop during the build phase |
| 3 | No formal risk management plan was documented — potential risks around competitor response, technical complexity, and adoption barriers were not explicitly addressed |
| 4 | No RACI matrix was created — roles were generally understood but decision-making authority and accountability were not formally defined across the team |
| 5 | Post-launch success metrics were not defined before launch — making it difficult to objectively measure performance and iterate based on results |

---

## My Recommendations to Improve Product Management

**Recommendation 1 — Create a Product Requirements Document (PRD)**  
Emily and Michael should have formalized the product scope in a PRD before development began. The mid-design addition of competitive features like national class competitions suggests scope was still evolving during build — a PRD would have locked the boundaries earlier and reduced the risk of scope creep.

**Recommendation 2 — Build a Continuous Customer Feedback Loop**  
The team did strong upfront research but customer engagement appears to have dropped off during the development phase. Weekly feedback touchpoints with educators and parents throughout the build — not just at the beginning and launch — would have validated design decisions in real time and reduced the risk of building features that missed the mark.

**Recommendation 3 — Define Success Metrics Before Launch**  
Clearly defined KPIs — such as student engagement rates, teacher adoption rates, learning outcome improvements, and revenue targets — should have been established at project kickoff. Without pre-defined metrics the team has no objective baseline to measure post-launch performance or guide the next iteration.

**Recommendation 4 — Create a RACI Matrix**  
The team had three distinct leaders with overlapping responsibilities — product vision, project execution, and marketing. A RACI matrix would have clarified who owned each decision, who needed to be consulted, and who was simply kept informed. This would have reduced coordination friction and made accountability explicit as the project scaled.

**Recommendation 5 — Invest in Formal Risk Management**  
With competitors actively entering the market, StreamLean needed a documented risk register covering competitive threats, technical complexity of multiplayer features, school adoption barriers, and budget risk. Identifying and planning for these risks upfront would have given the team contingency plans rather than having to react if problems emerged.

## My Biggest Insight

The StreamLean scenario shows that a product can launch successfully and still leave significant value on the table. The team did many things right — the right people, real market research, a clear vision, and strong execution. But the absence of formal scope documentation, a RACI, defined success metrics, and ongoing customer feedback means the company has no structured foundation for the next iteration. Success without these frameworks is hard to repeat. This is the core lesson of Course 1 — product management is not just about building something great the first time. It is about building the systems that make great outcomes repeatable and measurable at scale.
