## Lab Overview

This lab builds directly on the Five Forces and SWOT Analysis completed in the Five Forces Swot Analysis lab. Having assessed the market landscape for Time is Now LLC's Smartwatch, this lab shifts focus to evaluating the Product Manager's performance — analyzing which product management skills were applied effectively and which areas need improvement throughout the product's planning, development, and delivery lifecycle.

The Product Manager Skills Checklist covers skills, competencies, techniques, and required areas of expertise a product manager should possess — evaluated across three dimensions: what went well, what can be improved, and the rationale behind each assessment.

---

## Scenario Summary

Larry Madison is the Product Manager for Time is Now LLC's new Smartwatch. The product successfully launched and met management milestones - but the journey revealed both strong product management practices and notable gaps. Larry demonstrated strong meeting facilitation, market analysis, and schedule management. However, he struggled with financial expertise, feature prioritization, goal clarity, data quality, and workload management — each of which created friction, delays, or required intervention from others at critical points in the lifecycle.

---

## Skills Assessment

### Skills That Went Well

| Skill | Evidence from Scenario |
|---|---|
| Meeting facilitation & stakeholder preparation | Created agenda, defined expectations, gave stakeholders advance topics. Resulted in successful situation analysis |
| Market analysis & competitive research | Collaborated with Ram to apply Porter's Five Forces. Identified the rapid synching niche as a differentiator |
| Schedule management & work breakdown | Developed a realistic WBS, broke large work into smaller steps, met all management milestones |
| Cross-functional collaboration | Partnered with financial SME on IRR forecast, worked with Bethany to refine product concept |
| Prototype delivery | Managed pressure-filled development and delivered a prototype for review on time despite feature backtracking |

### Skills That Can Be Improved

| Skill | Evidence from Scenario |
|---|---|
| Financial acumen & ROI communication | Could not clearly explain investment costs and long-term financial benefits to management & required SME support |
| Feature prioritization | Concept identification meetings did not prioritize features. Larry had to backtrack during development to reprioritize |
| Goal clarity & SMART criteria | Bethany's goals were ambiguous, lacked relevancy, and were not measurable. Larry did not catch this earlier in the process |
| Data quality management | Business case delayed by outdated supplier data. Larry's team did not validate commercial database accuracy before use |
| Workload and task management | Too many tasks during delivery resulted in delayed actions. Larry did not delegate or escalate effectively |

*See `TDavis_ProductManager_Skills_Checklist.xlsx` for full rationale and detailed justification for each skill assessment.*

## Key Skills Demonstrated

- **Critical scenario analysis**  Evaluating a complete product lifecycle to identify both strengths and gaps in PM execution
- **Skills framework application** Using the Product Manager Skills Checklist to systematically assess competency across multiple dimensions
- **Evidence-based evaluation** Grounding every assessment in specific events from the scenario rather than general observations
- **Constructive feedback thinking** Identifying not just what went wrong but why it went wrong and what should have happened instead
- **Self-awareness** Connecting the scenario gaps to areas I am actively building in my own product management development

---

## Key Takeaway

What made this lab valuable was that Larry's story is realistic. He's a capable product manager who got most things right but had specific, identifiable blind spots that created avoidable problems. The financial acumen gap and the feature prioritization miss are the two that stand out most to me. Both represent situations where the product manager had the information available but didn't have the framework or the habit to act on it proactively. Its a skill I will focus on in my own development - building the discipline to apply the right framework at the right phase of the lifecycle before the gap becomes a problem, not after it causes one.
