# Lab: Develop Marketing Collateral — PR Plan
**Course:** IBM Product Manager Professional Certificate — Course 2  
**Module:** 3 — Team Communication & Public Relations  
**Lab:** Marketing Collateral & PR Plan — Time is Now LLC Smartwatch  
**Completed:** April 2026  

---

## Lab Overview

This lab introduced me to the role a product manager plays in supporting a product's go-to-market strategy through a structured Public Relations plan. The exercise builds on the Time is Now LLC Smartwatch scenario from previous labs - applying the market research and competitive analysis already completed to develop a real PR and marketing collateral plan with defined objectives, goals, target audience, schedule, and KPIs.

Before this lab I understood marketing as something that happens after a product is built. What I learned is that a product manager needs to think about how the product will be communicated to the market much earlier in the process and that the PR plan is one of the tools that connects product positioning to customer action.

---

## Scenario

Time is Now LLC is preparing to launch their new Smartwatch on **September 20, 2026**. The PR plan must be finalized and approved by **July 15, 2026** and the campaign must launch **30 days before** the product hits the market on **August 20, 2026**. The product's key differentiator is its rapid syncing feature and the target audience is Millennials and Gen X consumers looking for a functional, affordable alternative to premium smartwatch brands.

---

## My PR Plan

### 1. Objectives

- Launch the new Smartwatch by the beginning of Q4
- Contrast the Smartwatch against the competition and build a positive brand image
- Create a recognizable symbol or logo to differentiate the product
- Develop marketing collateral that resonates with the target audience
- Ensure materials are ready for events and tradeshows
- Develop compelling media to present the Smartwatch to the market
- Survey customers and gather real user experiences to share publicly
- Identify external advertising methods to reach the target audience

---

### 2. SMART Goals

| SMART Criteria | Goal |
|---|---|
| **Specific** | All media must clearly state the Smartwatch's features and differentiate it from competitors |
| **Measurable** | Accurately track sales, revenue, returns, complaints, and market share |
| **Achievable** | PR plan must be implemented at least 30 days before launch — all collateral in place by August 20, 2026 |
| **Relevant** | All media considerations must be relevant to the target audience and aligned with business objectives |
| **Time-Bound** | September 20, 2026 launch date is fixed — this plan must support that date |

---

### 3. Target Audience

Based on market research:

- **Primary audience** — Millennials aged 25–34 (highest smartwatch user segment)
- **Secondary audience** — Millennials aged 35–40 and Gen X customers aged 41–54

---

### 4. Schedule

| Milestone | Date |
|---|---|
| PR Plan finalized and approved | July 15, 2026 |
| PR Campaign launches | August 20, 2026 |
| Product Launch | September 20, 2026 |

---

### 5. Go-To-Market Strategy by Audience

| Audience | Strategy |
|---|---|
| Millennials 25–34 | Drive earned social buzz and peer validation |
| Millennials 35–40 | Build authority and trust through expert commentary |
| Gen X 41–54 | Establish credibility and long-term health legitimacy |

---

### 6. Media Plan — 4-Week Campaign

**Week 1 — Credibility Shock (Gen X & Older Millennials)**
- Break the master press release through major tech or business outlets such as Wired or Wall Street Journal using a predictive health angle to reach data-driven audiences
- Seed final production units to 5–10 authority figures - doctors, elite trainers, or productivity experts - to build trust with older segments before the social surge

**Week 2 — Social Surge (Younger & Older Millennials)**
- Launch TikTok and Instagram creator first-look videos
  - Ages 25–34: Focus on aesthetics and day-in-the-life utility
  - Ages 35–40: Focus on stress and schedule management benefits
- Run podcast sponsored segments in Productivity and Wellness shows targeting commuters and multitaskers

**Week 3 — Search & Review Lockdown (All Groups)**
- Lift review embargo simultaneously across YouTube deep-dives, Forbes Vetted, and CNET to create a search moat — when any audience searches for the watch they see 10+ positive sources at once
- Seed Facebook and LinkedIn community discussions and AMA sessions in health and tech groups for Gen X

**Week 4 — FOMO & Conversion Push (All Groups)**
- Deploy email marketing waitlist deadlines for older segments and countdown social content for younger audiences
- Run paid retargeting ads using PR coverage headlines as creative to reinforce wins at the decision moment

---

### 7. KPIs

- Sales
- Revenue
- Returns
- Complaints
- Market Share %

## Key Skills Demonstrated

- **PR and go-to-market planning** - structuring a product launch campaign with defined objectives, timeline, and audience strategy
- **SMART goal setting** - applying the SMART framework to make PR goals measurable and time-bound
- **Audience segmentation** - tailoring messaging and channel strategy to distinct customer groups based on research
- **Milestone and schedule planning** - working backward from a fixed launch date to build a realistic pre-launch timeline
- **KPI definition** - identifying the metrics that measure whether the PR plan achieved its business objectives

---

## Key Takeaway

My marketing degree gave me a strong foundation in market research, audience segmentation, and campaign planning — so the structure of this lab felt familiar. What was genuinely new was understanding where a product manager fits in the PR and go-to-market process. I came in assuming this was primarily the marketing team's responsibility. What this lab clarified is that a product manager needs to be close enough to the marketing strategy to ensure the product's value proposition is accurately and compellingly represented — not to execute the campaign themselves, but to make sure what gets communicated to the market actually reflects what the product delivers. That distinction between owning the execution and owning the accuracy of the message is something I hadn't thought about before. It also reinforced that the marketing instincts I already have are directly transferable to product management — particularly in how I communicate product value to stakeholders, frame product pitches, and present operational recommendations to leadership.
