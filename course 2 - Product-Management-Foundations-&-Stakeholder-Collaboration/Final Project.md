## Project Overview

The Course 2 Final Project brings together four exercises completed throughout the course and applies them to a new product scenario — the **Car Cam**, a dashboard camera targeting price-conscious consumers, parents of new drivers, and commercial customers including fleet, rideshare, and rental companies. The four exercises are:

1. Develop an Elevator Pitch for the Car Cam product
2. Apply Porter's Five Forces to assess the market and recommend strategies
3. Complete a Product Manager Skills Checklist evaluating which skills are required for this product
4. Develop an Initial Public Relations Plan for the Car Cam launch

## Exercise 1 — Elevator Pitch: Car Cam

The elevator pitch exercise required developing a concise product vision using the standard six-element format. This was the second time I applied this framework in Course 2 - the first being the Prep&Pivot nutrition app - and I found it easier to be specific the second time around because I understood what each element was really asking for.

| Element | Car Cam |
|---|---|
| **For** | Price-conscious consumers — especially parents of new teenage drivers - and commercial customers including rental car, fleet, and ride-share companies |
| **Who** | Want a dependable, adaptable car camera with reviewable footage to improve driving safety and provide reliable incident documentation |
| **Our Product** | The Car Cam |
| **That** | Provides drivers peace of mind through crash and reckless driving recordings, parked car monitoring, and road trip recording to enhance driving skills and capture usable footage |
| **Unlike** | Other automotive camera manufacturers that offer low quality hardware, restrictive mounting and storage options, and difficult portability |
| **Car Cam** | Delivers a high-quality camera with flexible mounting options, superior screen quality, and fast data transfer - at a lower cost than competitors |

## Exercise 2 — Porter's Five Forces Analysis: Car Cam

### Five Forces Assessment

| Force | Major Issues |
|---|---|
| **Entry** | Low technical barriers for basic dashcam hardware - many new entrants and copycat products. Moderate capital requirements for manufacturing and inventory. Moderate regulatory barriers around recording laws that can change frequently. Low distribution barriers due to e-commerce platforms |
| **Threat of Substitution** | Built-in camera systems on newer vehicles reduce the need for aftermarket devices. Smartphone apps can substitute for basic video capture |
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | Customers can easily switch between brands. Online reviews and ratings heavily influence purchase decisions. E-commerce gives competitors the ability to offer discounts and promotions that pressure margins |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | Supply chain volatility creates price instability for key components. Limited qualified manufacturers create quality consistency risk. Many components required to assemble the final product |
| **Rivalry Among Competitors** | Hardware differentiation is difficult. High marketing spend required to stand out, driving up customer acquisition costs. After-sales support and warranty costs create ongoing competitive pressure |

---

### Recommendations — Strategies to Address the Five Forces

| Major Issue | My Recommendations |
|---|---|
| **Threat of substitution from smartphones and built-in OEM systems** | Partner with commercial customers - fleet operators, rideshare companies, and insurance providers - where purpose-built reliability and incident documentation matter more than commodity video capture |
| **Low differentiation and intense price competition** | Focus on high-quality software features to enhance the customer experience. Offer tiered pricing with an optional subscription for advanced software features like AI incident detection and cloud backup |
| **High buyer power with high expectations and low switching costs** | Invest in product quality and post-purchase support. Reduce installation and UX friction with guided onboarding and one-tap evidence sharing |
| **High supplier power due to key component dependency and price volatility** | Negotiate supply agreements with locked-in forecasts. Dual-source critical components where possible. Design for component flexibility to reduce redesign risk |

## Exercise 4 — Initial Public Relations Plan: Car Cam

This exercise required developing a complete PR plan for the Car Cam launch - applying the same structured PR framework from the Smartwatch lab earlier in the course, but this time to a product with two very different target audiences that needed distinct messaging approaches.

### Objectives
- Launch the Car Cam on May 15, 2026
- Ensure all product and marketing materials carry the company logo consistently
- Develop media assets for events and tradeshows targeting both consumer and commercial audiences
- Gather customer feedback and user experience data post-launch
- Identify external advertising channels to reach target segments

### SMART Goals

| Criteria | Goal |
|---|---|
| **Specific** | All media must clearly state Car Cam's features and emphasize how it differs from competitors |
| **Measurable** | Track sales, revenue, customer satisfaction, customer retention, and market share % |
| **Achievable** | Materials ready for both commercial and non-commercial audiences before launch |
| **Relevant** | All media aligned to target audience needs and business objectives |
| **Time-Bound** | PR plan approved by March 15, 2026 — campaign launches April 15, 2026 — product launches May 15, 2026 |

### Target Audience

- **Non-commercial:** Adults aged 35–49 with emphasis on parents of new teenage drivers
- **Commercial:** Ride-share companies, fleet operators, and insurance providers

### Plan of Attack

- **Craft key messages** - highlight Car Cam's safety features, ease of use, and value for both families and commercial fleets
- **Media outreach** - pitch exclusive stories to automotive and tech journalists with embargoed product demos
- **Influencer partnerships** - provide early access units to trusted reviewers and parent and fleet influencers
- **Social media** - run a countdown campaign, share user stories, and host a launch-day livestream Q&A
- **Community engagement** - partner with local driving schools and insurance agencies for co-branded safety campaigns
- **Crisis preparedness** - prepare FAQ and holding statements for any potential product or shipping issues

### Media Area

- **Press kit** - press release, product fact sheet, high-resolution images, executive bios, and customer testimonials
- **Website media page** - dedicated section for journalists with downloadable assets and contact information
- **Demo videos** - product walkthroughs and safety feature highlights hosted on YouTube and the website
- **Spokesperson access** - offer interviews with the product manager and technical lead
- **Media list** - maintain and update a list of relevant journalists, bloggers, and influencers for ongoing outreach

### KPIs
Sales · Revenue · Customer Satisfaction · Customer Retention · Market Share %

## Key Takeaway

Completing the final project as a unified submission - elevator pitch, Five Forces analysis, skills checklist, and PR plan all applied to the same product - showed me for the first time how these frameworks connect to each other in practice. The elevator pitch forced me to define who the product is for and why they would choose it. The Five Forces analysis revealed the market pressures that could undermine that positioning. The skills checklist made me think about what capabilities a product manager actually needs to navigate those pressures. And the PR plan translated all of that thinking into a concrete launch strategy with timelines, audiences, and measurable outcomes. What I hadn't expected was how naturally the PR plan exercise connected to my marketing background. I understood the segmentation and channel strategy immediately, but what was new was seeing how a product manager uses that thinking to ensure the product story reaches the right people before launch, not just after. Doing all four in sequence made the full picture of what a product manager owns from vision through go-to-market feel concrete in a way that individual exercises hadn't.
